\section{Motivation}

\subsection{The fact:}
The raw poisoned examples are not easily detected by human labelers, the \textit{feature representations} of poisons are anomalous among the feature representations for data points with their (common) label.

This intuition leads to defense based on $k$ nearest neighbors($k$-NN) in ther feature space.

\subsection{Performance:}

Their initial defense detects nearly all of the poison instances without degrading overall performance.

\section{Baseline defenses against clean-label poisoning attacks}

\subsection{k-NN defense}

Take the plurality vote amongst the labels of a point's $k$ nearest neighbors in feature space. If the point's assigned label is not the mode amongst labels of the $k$ nearest neighbors, the point is discarded as being anomalous, and is not used in training the model.

\subsection{Baseline Defenses}

\colorbox{orange!15}{$L_2$ Defense}

Remove an $\epsilon>0$ fraction of points that are farthest in the feature space from the centroids of their classes.

\colorbox{orange!15}{Robust feature extractors}

Networks trained with adversarial training as a defense.

Drawbacks: slower training and reduced classification accuracy for non-adversarial inputs.

\subsection{One-class SVM defense}

It applies the one-class SVM algorithm to identify outliers in feature space for each label in the training set.

\subsection{Random Defense}

It filters out a random subset of all training data. If the poisoning attack is sensitive to poisons being removed, the random defense may be successful, {\color{red}at the cost of losing a proportionate amount of the unpoisoned training data.}
